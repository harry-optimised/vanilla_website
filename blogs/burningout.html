On Not Burning Out

20th January 2021

This is a short story of my personal experience. It is a reminder to myself that balance in life is important.

I deliberately called this post On Not Burning Out, because I don't believe I have ever fully burned out. But I have come close several times, and recently it happened again. Despite the experience being a stressful one, I consider myself lucky, because it served as a sharp reminder that I need to keep things in check, and it's only now that I have the time to think about and appreciate the lesson, that I realise how important it really is. It's so important, that I'm writing this post as a reminder, mostly for future me, but for others as well. We could all do with such a reminder every now and again.

I, like a lot of people, like to be ambitious and sometimes bite off more than I can chew. Last year was one of those times, and for the nine months from March to December 2020, I slowly committed to more and more and increased my involvement in those commitments.

At the end of November, it was beginning to burn me out, and things were starting to slip. The funny thing about committing to a lot is that when it's all going smoothly, you have the illusion of control, but when just one commitment starts to become volatile, you quickly realise how little capacity you have to absorb that volatility. One of my biggest commitments unexpectedly blew up in my face, and because I was so invested, I had no choice but to stick it out - at the expense of everything else. Had I not packed my time as fully as possible, I would have been fine, but instead, my other commitments floundered, my mental health suffered, and my energy and enthusiasm took a dive.

Fortunately, the Christmas break forced me to take a natural pause. I was surprised by how long it took me to recover, I didn't bounce back in the space of a few days, I went through several mental stages of recovery before I began feeling like myself again. So far this year, I have slowed my pace down just a little, and it has given me time to think and reflect on what I'm doing. Now I want to share a few benefits I've noticed in myself.

By Sean Stratton, Unsplash

By slowing down and reflecting on my experience as I go, I've noticed that I am better at what I do. I have time to just sit and consider what I'm trying to accomplish, I can read more around the topic, and think about it more deeply. Because I'm thinking more deeply, I have noticed that I have more spontaneous ideas, and those ideas are usually more creative and insightful. Ironically, this makes me do better and faster work than I would achieve by simply working harder.

I've become a nicer person. I have more patience for people and more compassion. I remember that towards the end of last year, when doing something as simple as going to the supermarket, I would get internally mad at people for being slow or blocking the aisle. I'd rationalise the response by telling myself that I worked really hard and therefore didn't deserve to have these idiots stress me out more than I already was. Of course, the world doesn't work like that. Now I just smile, it doesn't bother me. I've noticed that I'm more in control of my emotions when I have time to deal with them properly. All my relationships have benefitted as a result.

I'm happier, and I enjoy my work. I've rediscovered that craftsman-like passion for making things. I've remembered that the purpose of my life is not simply to produce and achieve as much as I can, at some point I should try and enjoy myself along the way, otherwise, what am I doing it all for? The funny thing is, when you're racing at a hundred miles an hour, you don't have time to remind yourself of such things when those are precisely the things you need reminding of most.

I am not a psychologist. I am not a mental health expert. I am not qualified to tell you how to live your life. I'm just one person who's slowly understanding the value of balance, and I hope I can inspire you to do the same.
