The Most Important Thing

16th December 2022

Turning thirty is one of those milestones that make a lot of people nervous. Like it's some sort of sacred threshold that says you're not young anymore - you better have your life figured out by now and be well on your way to success and fulfillment! As if...

I turn thirty in a few weeks and right on cue, I have begun to think about what I've done with my life... and what I want to do with it next. In this post I want to focus on one of the thoughts I've had, possibly the biggest one, that I have a finite amount of time left and that there is an opportunity cost to everything I do. At first glance this seems rather depressing, but it's not. It's actually quite clarifying and something I plan to pay attention to more often.

When I was younger, I thought I had all the time in the world, but as I've gotten older, I have become more aware of time passing. Naturally then, if I want to make the best use of my time, then that means that at any particular moment I should make sure that I'm doing the most important thing (whatever that is).

This is not a post about figuring out what that most important thing is, that's a difficult thing to do and I don't have an answer to it. Instead, this is a post about becoming aware of where I'm spending my time, because I've noticed that if I don't pay attention to it, I tend to waste it.

Introducing 'Balance'

I'm an engineer by training, so obviously I started by making a spreadsheet, which I have nicknamed 'Balance'. In short, Balance is a method for both tracking where I spend my time, and suggesting what I should maybe do next. Its purpose is to help me discover whether what I'm doing is time well spent. Let me briefly explain how it works.

I first define a set of personal values, which are things that are important to me, like building and creating things, health, or family. I can assign numbers to these values that capture how important a value is, relative to the others.

I then log activities that contribute to those values in some way. An activity can contribute towards multiple values, and can contribute a little, or a lot. The spreadsheet tracks how much I've contributed to each value over time, and begins to weight more heavily those values that seem to be getting neglected.

Activities are ranked by scoring them on how much they would contribute towards the values. For example, if I have a value called Health, then I could create an activity called Running that contributes positively towards it. If I don't do any physical activity, and assuming there are no other activities that contribute towards health, then over time the Health value will become more heavily weighted, and the Running activity will in turn become higher priority until I can't ignore it any longer.

Example spreadsheet. Notice how Run is ranked as highest priority because the health value is the most heavily weighted. All values are of the same importance in this example.

Experiments & Insights

Let me pause here and clarify that this was not about meticulously tracking everything I do, life is too messy for that. The point of the exercise was that it allowed me to express in fairly general terms that a set of values was important to me, and to capture, also in general terms, how where I spent my time contributed to fulfilling those values. I therefore set up a qualitative experiment.

What I hoped to achieve was to learn a little about myself. I was essentially declaring what I thought was important to me, and I assumed that I was already using my time effectively. I expected that I was naturally doing the things at the top of the list, and that my activities would generally line up with the spreadsheet. It turned out I was wrong.

Misalignment

The first thing I discovered was that the things I said were important, were not the things that I actually spent my time on. I originally down-weighted the importance of being social because I wanted to focus on activities such as finishing my degree and building up skills for my job. All the social activities were therefore near the bottom of the list, but I was doing them anyway.

I could figure out the actual importance I was assigning to being social by increasing it until the value weightings balanced out. It turned out it was of fairly high importance after all.

Given that what I was doing was misaligned with what I had declared was important, I wanted to figure out whether I was simply lying to myself about the initial importance of the values, or whether they were aspirational and the reality was that I was procrastinating.

Procrastinating

It turned out it was a mixture of both. Some values were poorly assigned to start with, and the early results challenged those importance weightings. It didn't take long for them to converge to stable values. After that, I assumed any deviations were because I was being lazy and defaulting to activities that were easier. I learned that my default activity was to read Instapaper articles.

Instapaper is a tool I use for collecting and organising blog posts. One of my values is Learning, and reading Instapaper articles directly contributes to it. On the surface it felt productive, and reading blog posts and technical articles is fun and interesting. Another of my values is my Mental Health, and one of my activities that contributes towards it is Meditation. The problem is that I find getting started with meditation quite difficult, so I don't do it very often.

It quickly became apparent that Instapaper was a crutch. As my activities contributed towards Learning, reading Instapaper articles became lower and lower priority. So what did I do in response? I simply bumped up the importance of Learning to counter it. I kept this up until the importance I had assigned to learning was ridiculously high. I took a step back and asked myself whether Learning was really five times more important than my mental health, four times more important than being healthy, and so on. I decided the answer was no, so I dialed the importance back down, and then promptly sat on a meditation cushion and tried to start clearing my mind.

The 'Balance' App

As the experiment continued, one of the major areas of friction was that constantly logging to a spreadsheet is tedious and time-consuming, even when using Google Sheets on mobile. What I needed was something faster, more user-friendly, and features like a timing mode that could log increments of activities.

I ended up building myself a mobile app that had these features so that I could log activities more easily. I built it with the Xano no-code tool and React Native, so it didn't take that long.

Actual screenshot from my Balance iOS app. It lets me log activities in one hour, half hour, and timed intervals.

The Most Important Thing

The experiment was both fun, and illuminating, and I'm still using the app to track my time. That being said, I don't run my life via spreadsheet, it's far too messy for that and the simple algorithm underlying the method isn't capable of modelling all the details that go into figuring out what is truly important in my current context.

The most important thing about this experiment then, is that it has made me conscious of how I spend my time. I said at the beginning that I'm becoming more aware of time passing, and that I would pay more attention to the opportunity cost of the activities I undertake. To that end, Balance is simply a tool that helps me do just that, and discover where those costs might lie. It challenges me to be thoughtful with my time, and I'm going to keep at it, for now.
